## 1. Four-Author Literary Challenge (for Liz)

A story told by four separate authors. Each fills a jigsaw-quadrant (like the Gartner quadrant). Each author's style is deliberately contrasting. The story itself is the same for all four. Four reasonable people — equal in importance, not necessarily equal in style. The *telling* is what matters, not the story. Each author's style reveals different facets. Only by reading all four (in any sequence) is the complete story understood.

**Note:** Framing is a "Broadway show international" — more specific, feels more real than a generic "stage show."

### The golf anecdote
"Golf is a good walk ruined." Shows there's no simple answer to any question. The subtlety of the question produces greatly different answers. But some questions aren't valid — they need to be thought about and refined before being asked.

### The Everest analogy
Getting to Everest — walking from the bottom vs. flying straight to the top. All the different stories in between pull out different aspects of the journey. It's not the destination but the journey that's more important and valuable.

All three illustrate the same core idea: the route, the telling, and the framing matter more than the endpoint.

## 4. Discussion: What Is Common Sense?

### The questions
- What is common sense?
- It depends on context
- It depends on experience
- It depends on how much you believe the whole deterministic argument
- It depends on what you actually are and where that came from

### The problem with common sense
- Common sense that is fantastic in one area does not translate to another
- Example: a street-wise criminal in New York — that common sense doesn't translate to living like an Eskimo at the North Pole
- Common sense can be taught
- Common sense shouldn't be trusted

### The limits of common sense
What does common sense tell you about:
- Travelling at the speed of light?
- Dealing with numbers the size of the universe?
- Something so small that an infinite number could almost be captured on a pinhead?

Common sense breaks down at the extremes of scale — the very large, the very small, the very fast. It is a local approximation, not a universal truth.

## 5. Discussion: Everyday Problems — The Broken Lift

### The scenario
You arrive for a meeting in a building on the 13th floor. The lift is broken. How do you solve this problem?

### First principle: it's not a problem
Nothing is a problem. Everything is an opportunity. Reframe it:

- An opportunity to get fit
- An opportunity to get your steps in
- An opportunity to help the old lady with her bag up the stairs
- An opportunity to impress your junior colleague with your fitness

### Then: lateral solutions (look tangentially at solving it)
A long list of approaches that don't just attack the obvious obstacle:

- Go to the building next door
- Catch their lift up to the 10th floor
- Cross via the connecting bridge
- Walk up the remaining 3 flights of stairs

### Or: rearrange the meeting
- Get the person on the 13th floor to come down and meet you on the ground floor

### The point
Everyday problems have multiple valid solutions. The first step is reframing — nothing is a problem, everything is an opportunity. Then look tangentially — don't just attack the obstacle head-on, look for lateral routes. And finally, rearrange the situation — sometimes the solution is to change the problem itself.

## 6. The Flip Side: Who Already Thinks This Way?

### The uncomfortable truth
The people who already operate on "nothing is a problem, only an opportunity" are the ones we consider bad actors:

- Criminals — who see an open window, an unlocked door, a car with keys in the ignition
- Despots and malign governments — who see opportunity to change laws in their interest
- Corrupt politicians — who see opportunity to produce tax breaks for their friends
- Politicians seeking bribes — who see opportunity to manipulate voters
- Social media influencers — who see opportunity to exploit attention
- Advertisers and marketers — who see opportunity to shape desire
- Petty criminals walking by your house — who see no one looking, an open window, keys in the car

### What do they have that you don't?
- They have **time**
- They have **motivation**
- They see **opportunity**

### What do you bring?
- You see **problems**
- You bring **pebbles, sticks, a blind eye, a shrug of the shoulders**

### The call to action
The bad actors are already using this mindset. The good actors are not. It's time to change — adopt the opportunity mindset for good, or keep losing to those who already have.

Time to change.

## 7. The 360 Questionnaire — Three-Tier Structure

### The goal
Ask AI to think — without deep research, using common sense — and generate questions for each sector in the beginner-level 360 assessment. What common-sense questions would any practitioner in those sectors ask and need to know?

### Tier 1 — Beginner (age 18)

In the best of all possible worlds, what should an 18-year-old know and have done?

- First aid course
- Learning to drive
- Riding a bike
- Going to the arts (theatre/gallery/concerts) — at least four times a year
- All the basic life skills
- Passing the citizenship test
- Basic ability to judge, and to learn something too

This is the foundation — the things any functioning adult should have under their belt before they enter the world properly.

### Tier 2 — Intermediate (age 25)

Roughly what a 25-year-old would have done by that age:

- Gone to university
- Left home
- Got their first job
- Got their second job
- Done some further studying
- Building independence, career, and life experience

### Tier 3 — Advanced / PhD level (age 35)

The kind of accumulated experience a 35-year-old would have:

- Got married
- Had children
- Deep professional expertise
- All the things that will have happened by that stage in life

### The design principle
The questionnaire maps to life stages. Each tier assumes a baseline of accumulated experience. The questions at each level should reflect what a person at that stage should reasonably know — and what common sense in their sector would demand they ask.

## 8. Practitioner Assessment Research — What Do They Actually Ask?

### The brief
Ask AI to research, for every type of practitioner involved at the beginner level, what they would actually assess. What questions, tests, and checks would a real practitioner use?

### The sports coach example
Take a sports coach working with middle- or long-distance runners:

**Before taking them on:**
- Initial assessment — fitness baseline, injury history, goals

**First day:**
- Assessment questions
- Physical tests

**Every session thereafter:**
- How did you sleep?
- How did you eat?
- Anything going on in your life I should know about?
- Wear a heart rate monitor

This is the daily/weekly rhythm of a real practitioner — the things they check every single time.

### Apply this to every practitioner type
- Some sectors will have multiple practitioner types that fit
- Research what each one actually does at intake, at first session, and at every subsequent session
- Build a basic questionnaire from this research

### The formula
Common sense + AI research = the questionnaire

### The minimum viable knowledge principle
The next way to look at it: what's the **least** we need to know?

There is a certain minimum that has to be divulged by the user and kept updated:
- When things change
- When they start a new activity or project

This is the baseline — the minimum viable information that any practitioner needs before they can begin, and that must be maintained as living data throughout.
