## 1. Four-Author Literary Challenge (for Liz)

A story told by four separate authors. Each fills a jigsaw-quadrant (like the Gartner quadrant). Each author's style is deliberately contrasting. The story itself is the same for all four. Four reasonable people — equal in importance, not necessarily equal in style. The *telling* is what matters, not the story. Each author's style reveals different facets. Only by reading all four (in any sequence) is the complete story understood.

**Note:** Framing is a "Broadway show international" — more specific, feels more real than a generic "stage show."

### The golf anecdote
"Golf is a good walk ruined." Shows there's no simple answer to any question. The subtlety of the question produces greatly different answers. But some questions aren't valid — they need to be thought about and refined before being asked.

### The Everest analogy
Getting to Everest — walking from the bottom vs. flying straight to the top. All the different stories in between pull out different aspects of the journey. It's not the destination but the journey that's more important and valuable.

All three illustrate the same core idea: the route, the telling, and the framing matter more than the endpoint.

### Translation into algorithm/program
This concept will be translated into an algorithm or a program or such like, and made available in and translated into many languages:
- English

- And a couple of other cool new languages

## 5. Discussion: Everyday Problems — The Broken Lift

### The scenario
You arrive for a meeting in a building on the 13th floor. The lift is broken. How do you solve this problem?

### First principle: it's not a problem
Nothing is a problem. Everything is an opportunity. Reframe it:

- An opportunity to get fit
- An opportunity to get your steps in
- An opportunity to help the old lady with her bag up the stairs
- An opportunity to impress your junior colleague with your fitness

### Then: lateral solutions (look tangentially at solving it)
A long list of approaches that don't just attack the obvious obstacle:

- Go to the building next door
- Catch their lift up to the 10th floor
- Cross via the connecting bridge
- Walk up the remaining 3 flights of stairs

### Or: rearrange the meeting
- Get the person on the 13th floor to come down and meet you on the ground floor

### The point
Everyday problems have multiple valid solutions. The first step is reframing — nothing is a problem, everything is an opportunity. Then look tangentially — don't just attack the obstacle head-on, look for lateral routes. And finally, rearrange the situation — sometimes the solution is to change the problem itself.

## 7. The 360 Questionnaire — Three-Tier Structure

### The goal
Ask AI to think — without deep research, using common sense — and generate questions for each sector in the beginner-level 360 assessment. What common-sense questions would any practitioner in those sectors ask and need to know?

### Tier 1 — Beginner (age 18)

In the best of all possible worlds, what should an 18-year-old know and have done?

- First aid course
- Learning to drive
- Riding a bike
- Going to the arts (theatre/gallery/concerts) — at least four times a year
- All the basic life skills
- Passing the citizenship test
- Basic ability to judge, and to learn something too

This is the foundation — the things any functioning adult should have under their belt before they enter the world properly.

### Tier 2 — Intermediate (age 25)

Roughly what a 25-year-old would have done by that age:

- Gone to university
- Left home
- Got their first job
- Got their second job
- Done some further studying
- Building independence, career, and life experience

### Tier 3 — Advanced / PhD level (age 35)

The kind of accumulated experience a 35-year-old would have:

- Got married
- Had children
- Deep professional expertise
- All the things that will have happened by that stage in life

### The design principle
The questionnaire maps to life stages. Each tier assumes a baseline of accumulated experience. The questions at each level should reflect what a person at that stage should reasonably know — and what common sense in their sector would demand they ask.

## 8. Practitioner Assessment Research — What Do They Actually Ask?

### The brief
Ask AI to research, for every type of practitioner involved at the beginner level, what they would actually assess. What questions, tests, and checks would a real practitioner use?

### The sports coach example
Take a sports coach working with middle- or long-distance runners:

**Before taking them on:**
- Initial assessment — fitness baseline, injury history, goals

**First day:**
- Assessment questions
- Physical tests

**Every session thereafter:**
- How did you sleep?
- How did you eat?
- Anything going on in your life I should know about?
- Wear a heart rate monitor

This is the daily/weekly rhythm of a real practitioner — the things they check every single time.

### Apply this to every practitioner type
- Some sectors will have multiple practitioner types that fit
- Research what each one actually does at intake, at first session, and at every subsequent session
- Build a basic questionnaire from this research

### The formula
Common sense + AI research = the questionnaire

### The minimum viable knowledge principle
The next way to look at it: what's the **least** we need to know?

There is a certain minimum that has to be divulged by the user and kept updated:
- When things change
- When they start a new activity or project

This is the baseline — the minimum viable information that any practitioner needs before they can begin, and that must be maintained as living data throughout.

## 12. Riding on the Backs of Others — Our Role, Our Limits, Our Guardrails

### The blessing and the curse of familiarity
What we are offering should sound familiar. This is both a **blessing** and a **curse**.

The blessing: people recognise the shape of it. It makes intuitive sense.

The curse: they bring their Bayesian priors — their past experiences with similar-sounding things, their misinformation, their scars from bad practice. We have to work in the real world where humans are imperfectly built, imperfect in use, and subject to malign influence.

### We ride on the backs of others
We are not academic researchers. We are not research scientists. We ride on the backs of those who are.

Our role is to:
- Utilise all the good knowledge that has come before
- Rearrange it
- Integrate it
- Simplify it
- Smooth off the rough edges
- Ensure, like a jigsaw puzzle, it fits into a holistic whole

The argument must stand up from top to bottom — not just theoretically, but practically and pragmatically.

### The NICE analogy
We function like NICE (the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence) — the body that analyses all the tools, medicines, and practices available and determines which ones improve the health of the nation.

Some things don't work. Some are too expensive. Some duplicate others. Some do the main thing well but have side effects or contraindications that make them risky.

We need a **standards body** that:
- Approves certain medicines and practices
- Tests them — there are gems to be found
- Validates that past decisions remain true
- Tries and tests innovative new ideas

The end result: a **body of work** that approves or supports certain methods, certain practices, certain medicines. This is the function we need to serve.

### The human problem
We cannot ignore the human element. People:
- Have **Bayesian priors** — prior beliefs shaped by everything they've experienced
- Have been **misinformed** — by bad actors, bad systems, bad luck
- Have been damaged by **bad practices** — from humans and institutions
- Are **imperfectly built** and **imperfect in use**
- Are subject to **malign influence** — as covered in §6 and §10

### The guardrails
They need to be:
- **Guided** — not left to drift
- **Walled** — boundaries imposed, rules set
- **Policed** — and the police themselves must be subject to a **court system**

This is the hard truth: even the most elegant, effective, honest, and simple system — in spite of humans — must still deliver **for humans** an effective solution.

The system must work *through* human imperfection, not pretend it doesn't exist.

## 13. Systems Integration — Components, Foundations, and Full-Spectrum Defence

### Many components, one integrator
There are many components to our system. Each one is self-contained. Our role is to be the **systems integrator** — ensuring that it integrates, that it connects.

### The black box principle
Each component is a black box. It must be:
- Tested **initially** — to ensure it performs as intended
- Tested **ongoingly** — to ensure it continues to perform as intended
- Held to a standard of **improvement over time** — a positive feedback loop

A component that doesn't improve is a component that decays.

### Necessity and sufficiency
Many components, but each must be **necessary** — not more, not less. In totality they must be **sufficient** — nothing missing, nothing superfluous.

What we must avoid:
- Introducing **complexity** unnecessarily
- Making the system **fragile** — brittle to change
- Building on foundations that turn to **sand** — unstable, unreliable
- Leaving anything **out** — incomplete is broken

### The two layers
The **foundations** are important. But so is the **layer of synthesis** — the integration, the connections, the emergent whole that is greater than the sum of the components.

### The full ecosystem
And now we need the surrounding functions:

| Layer | Function |
|-------|----------|
| **Approval** | Standards, validation, the standards body from §12 |
| **Policing** | Guardrails, rules, the court system from §12 |
| **Marketing** | Telling the story, attracting users |
| **Education** | Teaching users, practitioners, the public |

### Full-spectrum funding
Funding isn't just for what enhances and improves. It must also fund:

- Those things that **defend** — protect the system, its users, its integrity

You cannot build a wall and leave the sappers unchallenged. The system must have both a defensive capability and an offensive one — not for aggression, but for survival. The rich man from §10, the malign influences from §6 — they won't stop. Neither can we.

## 16. Supportive Space — The Metamorphosis Environment

### The addiction recovery analogy
If you're an alcoholic trying to give up and go clean, the standard advice is:
- **Disassociate** from the other alcoholics — the friends who supported you in your bad life
- **Avoid** those places that were part of your old bad life

You cannot change in the same environment that enabled the old you. You need a **new space**.

### What we must provide
Our organisation must provide:
- **A space** that is supportive and understanding
- **A friend group** — a new community that reinforces the new direction, not the old one

For someone trying to change — to metamorphose from their bad state into their new state — isolation from the old is necessary but not sufficient. You must also provide the *new*. The space, the people, the structure.

### The metamorphosis
The process is not just "stop doing X." It is:
- Let go of the old environment
- Let go of the old friends who enabled it
- Find a new space — ours
- Find a new community — ours
- Metamorphose from bad state into new

### The connection to §14
This is the organisational parallel to Take Ownership. §14 says: treat the park like it's yours, treat the customer like a friend. §16 says: be the friend. Be the park. Build the space that someone needs when they've walked away from everything that was holding them back.

Walking away means walking away from friends, from habits, from comfort. We need to build what replaces it all.

## 24. The Wedding Planner and the Unplanned Life

They've got to this point in life by stumbling — the way that society led them, or their parents, or their friends, or opportunity, or luck, or chance, or fate. They have no idea where they're going and where they want to go. And if they did even know where they wanted to go, they wouldn't know how to get there.

People plan almost everything. A wedding — so important, so valuable, so complex, so expensive — they often employ a wedding planner who's done that sort of thing before, who's experienced, who can be focused, who provides resources, the time to manage the situation, to resolve it into a satisfactory conclusion.
